On July 21st, 1969, humanity achieved something that shook the world and changed the course of history...

Timeline

Pre-Launch

Early 1960s: Due to the increasing tensions caused by the cold war, the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) begins the Apollo program. The goal - land a man on the moon and safely return him to Earth before the decade's end.

This was set by John F .Kennedy himself during an address to the United States Congress.

Launch Day

July 16th, 1969: Launch Day. After close to a decade of testing, setbacks and successes, Apollo 11 was ready, and at 9:32am it was launched from Kennedy Space Center in Florida aboard the saturn V rocket.

Its crew: Neil Armstrong (Commander), Edwin E. "Buzz" Aldrin Jr. (Lunar Module Pilot) and Michael Collins (Command Module Pilot).

Arrival

July 19th, 1969: After three days of travelling through the vast void of space, Apollo 11 enters lunar orbit, and preperations begin to land on the moon.

The Moon Landing

July 20th, 1969: With preperations complete, Neil Armstrong and Edwin E. Aldrin Jr. begin the descent to the Moon's surface, and at 4:17 PM EDT: the lunar module, codenamed The Eagle, lands on the Moon in the Sea of Tranquility.

That's one small step for (a) man...

10:56 PM EDT: Six hours after landing, Neil Armstrong slowly exits the Lunar Module and steps onto the surface of the Moon, delivering the quote that will forever echo throughout history.

"That's one small step for [a] man, one giant leap for mankind."

At 11:15 PM EDT Edwin E. Aldrin Jr. joins his commander on the surface, quoting the Moon to be that of "Magnificant desolation".

Together, they proceed to complete their moonwalk activities, which include collecting samples, taking photographs and setting up scientific experiments.

And then at 1:54 PM EDT, a mere 21 and a half hours later, the Lunar Module ascends from the lunar surface and rejoins Michael Collins in the Command Module that stayed orbiting the Moon.

One giant leap for mankind

After spending a day and a half in lunar orbit and completeing various tasks, Apollo 11 begins its journey back to Earth, and on July 24th, 1969 Apollo 11 splashes down in the Pacific Ocean.

The crew are recovered by the USS Hornet and immediately placed into quaratine to ensure they haven't brought back any harmful pathogens. And on August 13th, 1969 the three astronauts are released from quarantine. All in good health.

This mission was a monumental achievement in both human space exploration and human history. Achieving President John F. Kennedy's goal, and significantly advancing scientific understanding of the moon.

Although the astronauts became the face and imagery of the landing, and rightly so, at its peak, 400,000 people worked on this mission. Without their sacrifices, knowledge and determination, the world as we know it may have looked vastly different.

So as you look through this historical document and visit the gallery pages, share a thought for those working behind the scenes, who helped bring this moment together.

To them, I say thank you.
